Val Kilmer, the acclaimed actor best known for his roles in Top Gun, The Doors, Batman Forever and Tombstone, will appear posthumously in a new independent film called As Deep as the Grave through an AI-generated version of his likeness and voice, the production company announced this week. Kilmer, who died in April 2025 after a prolonged battle with throat cancer, had been cast in the film years before his death but was never well enough to film his scenes. Director Coerte Voorhees has now realised his original vision using state-of-the-art generative AI, with the full cooperation of Kilmer's estate and his daughter Mercedes.

Kilmer was cast as Father Fintan, a Catholic priest with deep ties to Native American spirituality, in a film based on the true story of archaeologists Ann and Earl Morris, whose excavations in Arizona uncovered significant Native American history. The ensemble cast includes Abigail Lawrie, Tom Felton, Wes Studi, and Abigail Breslin. The AI production relied on SAG-AFTRA guidelines covering posthumous digital performances, and Kilmer's estate was compensated for his appearance in the film. Voorhees said in a statement that he had always believed the project required Kilmer's presence and that modern technology had finally made it possible to honour that original casting decision.

Mercedes Kilmer, who gave her consent for the AI portrayal, said her father had been deeply drawn to the project from the outset because of his personal spiritual nature and because the film's themes of discovery and enlightenment in the American Southwest resonated with him. "He always looked at emerging technologies with optimism as a tool to expand the possibilities of storytelling," she said, offering a personal framing that placed the AI decision within a characterological consistency rather than presenting it as a compromise. The estate's active involvement in the production is notable at a time when the ethics of posthumous digital performance remain intensely debated in the entertainment industry.

The film, which has been in post-production for three years, is expected to be released later in 2026. The announcement was greeted with a divided public response: some fans and industry professionals praised the project's care in securing family consent and following union guidelines, while others described the use of AI to recreate a deceased actor's performance as an ethical line that should not be crossed regardless of consent. Critics on social media used phrases such as "it should be illegal," reflecting the widespread discomfort with the technology's potential to commodify the image and voice of the deceased. The debate around As Deep as the Grave is likely to intensify as release approaches and audiences see the AI performance in context for the first time.
