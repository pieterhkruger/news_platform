Palaeontologists working in the Triassic rock formations of southern Brazil have announced the discovery of a dinosaur species that appears to share distinctive skeletal features with early dinosaur lineages previously found only in Europe, adding new evidence to the theory that dinosaurs emerged from a common ancestral population that ranged across the supercontinent Pangaea before its fragmentation. The creature, named Gondwanax paranaensis, was a small bipedal predator that lived approximately 230 million years ago and is described in a paper published in the journal Nature Communications.

The significance of the find lies in the combination of anatomical features it displays. Researchers identified characteristics of the pelvis, hind limbs and ankle joint that closely resemble those found in early dinosauriform fossils excavated in Poland, Germany and Scotland. Prior to this discovery, such features had not been documented in South American specimens of equivalent age, leading some researchers to propose that the European and South American lineages diverged earlier than the fossil record suggested.

Lead researcher Dr. Rodrigo Müller of the Federal University of Santa Maria said the discovery supported a model of early dinosaur evolution in which a cosmopolitan ancestral population radiated outward from a single origin point across the landmass of Pangaea, with regional variation developing as the supercontinent gradually broke apart. The Brazil-Europe anatomical connection does not imply direct migration between the two modern landmasses, but rather that both regions were part of the same interconnected ecosystem during the Triassic period, before the Atlantic Ocean existed.

The fossil was uncovered during routine excavation at a site in the Rio Grande do Sul state that has yielded several significant Triassic specimens over the past two decades. The remote area has become one of the most productive zones for early dinosaur research in the world, offering exceptional preservation conditions. Müller said the team planned further excavation at the site in search of additional specimens that might clarify the evolutionary relationships between Gondwanax paranaensis and related species across both hemispheres.
