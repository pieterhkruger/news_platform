Micron Technology reported second-quarter revenue of $23.86 billion on Wednesday, sharply exceeding Wall Street expectations of approximately $20 billion and representing a staggering 196 percent increase compared with the same period the previous year, driven by explosive demand for memory chips used in artificial intelligence systems. The company also forecast third-quarter revenue of around $33.5 billion, well above consensus estimates, pointing to continued acceleration of AI-driven memory demand as data centre customers race to expand computing capacity for AI training and inference workloads.

Despite the exceptional financial performance, Micron's shares fell around 4 percent in after-hours trading after the company announced a revised 2026 capital expenditure plan of more than $25 billion, an increase of approximately $5 billion from its earlier guidance. The market's muted reaction illustrated the dynamic facing major semiconductor capital spenders: even record revenue growth is being accompanied by enormous investment requirements to keep pace with rapidly escalating demand. Some analysts characterised the capex expansion as a necessary and confident signal from management, while others flagged the rising supply risk that comes with aggressive capacity additions across the industry.

Micron's performance is being driven primarily by demand for high-bandwidth memory, the specialised chips used in Nvidia's AI accelerators and those of competing chip designers such as AMD. HBM has emerged as one of the most strategically critical products in the semiconductor supply chain, with Micron competing alongside SK Hynix and Samsung to supply the memory bandwidth that underpins large language model inference and the next generation of agentic AI systems. Micron said that customers are committing to long-term data centre investments as technology companies across the industry race toward artificial general intelligence, creating multi-year demand visibility that justifies its expanded capital programme.

The company's board also approved a 30 percent increase to its quarterly dividend, an unusual gesture of shareholder confidence in the context of a major capex escalation. Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra framed the spending increase as essential to capturing a structural demand shift that he described as generational in scale. He noted that the company's technology roadmap was tracking ahead of expectations, with next-generation HBM4 qualification on schedule and advanced packaging capabilities being expanded at multiple production sites.

The results reinforce the view that the memory industry has entered a prolonged upcycle, driven not by the cyclical swings in consumer electronics demand that characterised previous memory booms, but by the sustained and growing appetite of hyperscale data centre operators for AI compute capacity. Analysts at several major investment banks raised their price targets for Micron following the results, citing the combination of above-consensus revenue guidance, margin expansion, and the company's strong position in HBM as reasons for continued optimism. The main risk flagged by bears remains an overcapacity scenario in the second half of 2027 if all three major HBM producers simultaneously bring large volumes of new capacity online.
