Paul Freud, the 66-year-old British painter and son of the celebrated figurative artist Lucian Freud, has said in a new interview that art has become an essential tool for him in making sense of a world shaped increasingly by war, political extremism, and humanitarian crisis. Freud, who has spent much of his career working in relative obscurity given the towering reputations of his father and great-grandfather Sigmund Freud, has found a new public voice through a body of work that confronts contemporary conflict and suffering with an unflinching directness.

The most striking recent expression of this approach is his Genocide Series, a group of portraits that have attracted significant critical attention since being shown at the London Art Fair in January under the collective title Madonna and Child. The series consists of three large-format portraits depicting women holding skeletal children, imagery drawn directly from photographic documentation of the ongoing famine in Gaza. Freud said in the interview that he was drawn to the subject because conventional language and political discourse had failed to communicate the scale of the suffering, and that painting — with its slowness, its insistence on the physical presence of the body, and its long art-historical dialogue with depictions of grief — could reach places that journalism and advocacy could not.

Freud describes his artistic practice as rooted in portraiture, but not in portraiture understood as likeness or record. "Faces become landscapes for inner states," he has said, a formulation that echoes his father Lucian's preoccupation with the psychological weight of the painted human figure. Paul Freud has, however, developed a distinct political and social vocabulary that sets him apart from Lucian's more interior and domestic subject matter. Where Lucian's subjects were often nude, singular, and caught in a moment of psychological exposure, Paul's are clothed, contextualised by history and politics, and deliberately placed in dialogue with iconic images from the Western art tradition.

The artist is currently in the final stages of planning a major exhibition in Beijing, organised in collaboration with prominent Chinese curator Xiaojuan Sun and facilitated by collector and arts organiser Qiongzi Zhu. The exhibition will be the first significant showing of Freud's work in China and represents an unusual cultural moment, given the contemporary political sensitivities around Western art that engages with issues of war and sovereignty. Freud has said he is approaching the Beijing project with the same directness he brings to his work generally, and that he believes the themes of the paintings — loss, state power, the vulnerability of civilians — have a universal resonance that transcends specific cultural contexts.

Freud has spoken about the challenge of working in the long shadow of two of the twentieth century's most famous names. He has acknowledged that comparisons with his father and great-grandfather are inevitable but has argued that they have ultimately been more clarifying than limiting, forcing him to articulate precisely what he is doing that is different. His work has been shown at the Cole-Levi Klimt gallery in London and at international art fairs, and critical reception has been increasingly warm as the political dimension of the paintings has found a receptive audience in a moment of acute global upheaval.
