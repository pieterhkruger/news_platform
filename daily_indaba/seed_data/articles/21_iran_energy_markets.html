The escalating US-Israeli war against Iran, now in its third week as of 15 March 2026, has triggered severe disruptions across global energy markets and heightened security concerns throughout the Middle East. The conflict, which began with surprise strikes on 28 February that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and other senior officials, has seen Iran retaliate by effectively closing the Strait of Hormuz to most commercial traffic through attacks on tankers and threats to shipping. This chokepoint, through which roughly one-fifth of the world's oil and a significant share of liquefied natural gas flows, has seen transit volumes plummet, leading to the suspension of approximately 20 percent of global crude and natural gas supply. Oil prices have surged dramatically in response, with Brent crude climbing above $100 per barrel in recent sessions—reaching highs not seen since 2022—and at times approaching $120 amid fears of prolonged disruption. The spike has rippled through fuel costs worldwide, pushing up petrol prices in major markets and contributing to inflationary pressures. Analysts warn that a sustained blockade or further damage to infrastructure could push prices even higher, potentially to $150 or more in extreme scenarios, with recovery likely to take weeks or months even if hostilities subside quickly. Regional security has deteriorated sharply, as Iran has extended retaliatory strikes to Gulf states hosting US bases, targeting energy facilities and maritime assets in countries including Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Oman, and others. Air defenses in the Gulf have intercepted many projectiles, but isolated impacts have occurred, including on industrial zones and infrastructure. The conflict has also drawn in proxies, with Hezbollah launching attacks on Israel and broader instability affecting neighboring areas such as Lebanon. US and Israeli forces continue wide-scale strikes on Iranian military, nuclear-related, and regime infrastructure, while Tehran vows to maintain pressure on shipping and regional targets. The energy shock has prompted international responses, including coordinated releases from strategic petroleum reserves by bodies such as the IEA, though these have so far failed to reverse price gains. Political risk insurance for shipping has risen sharply, and calls have grown for multinational efforts to secure the Strait, though reluctance persists among allies to engage directly in escort operations. With no clear end in sight and leadership in Tehran shifting to Mojtaba Khamenei—signaling continuity of hardline policies—the outlook remains uncertain, posing risks to global economic stability and regional peace.
