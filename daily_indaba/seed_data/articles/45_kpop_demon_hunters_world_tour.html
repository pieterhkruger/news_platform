Netflix is in advanced negotiations with major concert promoters to stage a global live tour based on KPop Demon Hunters, the animated film that has become the streaming platform's most-watched original movie of all time, according to sources briefed on the discussions. The planned tour would capitalise on the film's extraordinary musical success, with songs from the soundtrack having topped the Billboard charts and become a global cultural phenomenon since the film's debut in June 2025.

KPop Demon Hunters, which follows three Korean pop stars who secretly moonlight as demon hunters, won the Oscar for Best Animated Feature at this year's Academy Awards ceremony, while its breakout anthem Golden also claimed the Oscar for Best Original Song — the first time a K-pop track has ever won that award. The film has amassed over 500 million views worldwide, making it not only Netflix's most successful original movie but one of the most-watched animated feature films in streaming history. The Oscar wins accelerated an already remarkable cultural moment for the film, driving a second wave of viewing and renewed demand for live experiences.

According to people familiar with the talks, Netflix is negotiating to book concert venues with capacities of between 10,000 and 20,000 seats, with dozens of stops envisioned across Asia, North America, and Europe. The streaming company is aiming to launch the tour in 2026, timed to precede the release of an animated sequel to the film. The tour is seen by Netflix executives as a way to sustain and extend the franchise's cultural footprint, diversifying the platform's revenue beyond subscriptions and into the live entertainment space that has proven highly lucrative for other intellectual property owners.

The question of who will perform on tour remains under active discussion. The HUNTR/X trio — comprising performers Ejae, Audrey Nuna, and Rei Ami, who provided the voices and musical performances for the film's three main characters — are potentially set to take the stage in some cities. However, sources indicated that Netflix is also exploring the possibility of holographic or virtual performers representing the animated characters themselves, a format that could be scaled more efficiently across multiple cities and has precedent in the K-pop industry's existing experiments with virtual artists. No final decision on the performance format has been announced.
