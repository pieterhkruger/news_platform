The United States government has withdrawn a sweeping proposed rule that would have imposed strict country-tier restrictions on the export of advanced AI chips, reversing a policy approach that had alarmed technology companies, allied governments and academic institutions since its announcement several months ago. The Commerce Department confirmed that the tiered licensing framework, which would have placed most countries outside a small group of close US allies in a restricted category with annual chip import limits, would not proceed in its current form. Officials said a revised approach was being developed that would be more targeted and less disruptive to global technology supply chains.

The proposed rule had drawn fierce opposition from the semiconductor industry, which argued that the restrictions would accelerate efforts by China and other countries to develop domestic chip manufacturing capabilities while simultaneously undermining American companies' commercial relationships with customers in Europe, Southeast Asia and Latin America. Nvidia, the dominant supplier of AI training chips, had warned that the restrictions would cost it billions of dollars in lost revenue and could ultimately weaken US leadership in AI development by pushing international customers toward alternative suppliers.

Allied governments, including those of the Netherlands, Japan, South Korea and Germany, had also registered strong objections through diplomatic channels. Several expressed concern that the restrictions would limit access to AI technology for legitimate research and commercial purposes, creating a two-tier innovation environment that would disadvantage their domestic industries without meaningfully improving US national security. The Dutch government, home to ASML, the only manufacturer of extreme ultraviolet chip-making equipment, had been particularly vocal in its opposition.

The withdrawal does not mean that AI chip export controls are being abandoned entirely. Officials indicated that a more surgical framework is being developed, likely focused on preventing specific high-capability chips from reaching military end-users in designated countries rather than applying broad country-level restrictions. Technology policy analysts said the reversal reflected the limits of export control as a tool for managing AI competition when the technology is deeply embedded in global commercial supply chains that cannot be easily segmented.
