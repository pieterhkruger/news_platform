A team of researchers at the University of Valladolid in Spain has developed an artificial cornea derived from collagen extracted from the scales of farmed fish, a breakthrough that could offer a low-cost, widely available alternative to human donor tissue for patients with corneal blindness in low- and middle-income countries. The study, published in the journal Advanced Healthcare Materials, describes a bioengineering process that converts fish-scale collagen into a transparent, structurally stable material that can be shaped to match the curvature of a human cornea and implanted using standard surgical techniques.

Corneal disease is one of the leading causes of blindness globally, affecting an estimated twelve million people who are awaiting corneal transplants. The shortage of donor tissue is most acute in parts of sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia and Latin America, where eye banks operate at a fraction of the capacity needed to meet clinical demand. The Spanish team's approach addresses the supply problem by drawing on a material that is abundant, cheap and currently discarded as a by-product of the fish processing industry.

In laboratory trials, the fish-scale corneal implant demonstrated transparency comparable to human donor tissue and supported the growth of corneal epithelial cells, the outer layer of the cornea that is critical for normal vision and the eye's protective function. The material also showed adequate mechanical strength in stress-testing simulations designed to replicate the forces exerted on the cornea during normal eye movement and blinking. The research team noted that fish-scale collagen is structurally similar to human collagen at the molecular level, which they believe explains its biocompatibility.

Clinical trials in human patients are expected to begin within the next two years, pending regulatory approval in Spain and potentially in partner countries in Africa and Asia where the unmet need is greatest. Ophthalmologists not involved in the study described the research as promising but cautioned that the transition from laboratory success to clinical use involves significant additional testing. They noted that the cornea's optical properties must be verified under real physiological conditions and that long-term outcomes, including immune rejection rates, would need to be tracked in controlled trials before the implant could be considered for widespread use.
