Glencore's South African ferrochrome unit could walk away from government-led rescue talks over the terms of a heavily discounted electricity offer that the mining giant says are not acceptable in their current form, a company executive warned on Thursday. The statement by Glencore Ferroalloys CEO Japie Fullard, made at a mining industry conference in Johannesburg, sent a sharp signal to the government that the framework conditions underpinning the deal remain contested despite weeks of negotiations.

Eskom, the state-owned power utility, offered South Africa's two largest ferrochrome producers, including Glencore's local unit and Samancor Chrome, steeply discounted electricity tariffs in late February, cutting the rate from R1.36 to 62 cents per kilowatt hour. The offer, contingent on approval by the National Energy Regulator of South Africa, was presented as a bid to prevent the near-total collapse of the ferrochrome smelting sector, which has shed the majority of its productive capacity over the past decade under the combined pressure of high electricity prices and intensifying competition from Chinese producers. However, neither Glencore nor Samancor have disclosed the specific conditions attached to the discount, and negotiations on those conditions are continuing.

"The terms and conditions, the way that it is now, I unfortunately will not be in a position to sign," Fullard told delegates at the conference, adding that certain elements of the package were not acceptable to the company. He did not specify which conditions were most problematic, but industry sources indicated that concerns may relate to commitments around production volumes, employment levels, and the duration of the preferential rate. Glencore is said to be seeking greater certainty on pricing over a longer horizon to justify the capital expenditure required to restart idled smelter capacity.

The stakes are high. South Africa once had 66 ferrochrome smelters in operation; today only 11 remain active. The sector, centred on Limpopo province's bushveld region, is a significant employer and an important contributor to South Africa's chromite ore export revenues. The collapse of additional smelting capacity would cost thousands of jobs and eliminate value-added processing that generates better export returns than raw ore alone. Industry analysts estimate that a viable ferrochrome smelting sector requires electricity at no more than 60 to 70 cents per kilowatt hour to compete with Chinese producers, who benefit from subsidised coal-fired power.

The Department of Trade, Industry and Competition and the Department of Mineral and Petroleum Resources, which have been involved in mediating the rescue framework, did not immediately respond to requests for comment on Fullard's remarks. The mining industry's lobbying body, the Minerals Council South Africa, called for urgent progress on reaching a workable agreement before more capacity is permanently decommissioned. The outcome of the Glencore talks is being watched closely by other energy-intensive industries, including aluminium and paper manufacturing, that may be in line for similar support packages should a precedent be set.
