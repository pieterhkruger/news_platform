The US Food and Drug Administration said on Dec. 2 that it had issued draft guidance aimed at reducing the use of non-human primates in safety studies for certain experimental monoclonal antibodies. The move does not end animal testing across drug development, but it does narrow when lengthy primate studies are expected before first-in-human trials for some monospecific antibody products.

Under the draft framework, companies can use a weight-of-evidence approach to decide whether six-month repeat-dose toxicity studies in primates are necessary. The FDA said sponsors should consider factors such as the biology of the target, what is already known from related antibodies, tissue cross-reactivity data, shorter studies and other nonclinical evidence. In cases where those signals are well understood, the agency said long primate studies may be reduced or omitted.

The FDA cast the change as both a scientific and practical adjustment. According to Reuters and the agency's own materials, a typical preclinical monoclonal-antibody programme can involve more than 100 non-human primates at a cost of roughly $50,000 per animal. Commissioner Marty Makary said newer methods now offer more effective and humane ways to evaluate drug safety while potentially cutting development time and research costs.

The December draft guidance builds on the broader roadmap the FDA released in April 2025 to reduce animal testing in preclinical safety studies. That earlier plan highlighted approaches such as computational modelling, organoids and organ-on-a-chip systems, and set out a longer transition away from older default testing practices. The December document is more targeted, focusing on a well-defined class of antibody drugs where the agency believes existing science can support a lighter primate-testing burden.

The guidance is still only a draft, and the FDA invited public comment before finalising it. For drug developers, that means the change is best understood as a regulatory opening rather than a blanket exemption. But it is still a meaningful shift: the agency is explicitly signalling that, for some antibody programmes, primate testing should no longer be treated as automatic when other evidence can answer the same safety questions.
