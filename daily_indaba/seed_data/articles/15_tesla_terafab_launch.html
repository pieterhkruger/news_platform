Tesla has announced that its Terafab AI chip manufacturing project, a custom silicon initiative the company has been developing for several years, will begin commercial-scale production within days. The announcement, made by chief executive Elon Musk during an online presentation to investors, marks a significant step in Tesla's strategy to reduce its dependence on third-party chip suppliers such as Nvidia for the AI compute that underpins its autonomous driving systems, humanoid robotics programme and Dojo supercomputing infrastructure. Musk said the first production run of Terafab chips would be allocated internally to Tesla's own AI training workloads before any excess capacity was offered to external customers.

Tesla has been developing its own custom AI silicon since at least 2016, initially for inference tasks in its vehicles before expanding the ambition to training-scale compute. The Dojo supercomputer, which Tesla announced in 2021, was the first public demonstration of the company's in-house chip capabilities. Terafab represents a substantial leap in manufacturing scale and performance, with Musk claiming that the architecture delivers better performance per watt than competing solutions for the specific workloads involved in training large-scale vision and language models.

Industry analysts reacted with measured interest, noting that Tesla has a history of announcing hardware milestones on aggressive timelines. Several observers questioned whether Terafab's production volumes would be sufficient to meaningfully reduce Tesla's external chip spending in the near term, particularly given the scale of compute required for training frontier AI models. The announcement did, however, appear to influence sentiment toward Nvidia's shares, which declined modestly in after-hours trading as investors assessed whether Tesla represented a meaningful competitive threat in the custom chip market.

Beyond Tesla's own use, Musk indicated that the company intended to offer Terafab compute capacity to selected external customers as part of an AI infrastructure service. This would position Tesla more directly in competition with established cloud providers and AI infrastructure companies. Analysts said the commercial viability of this approach would depend heavily on the price and performance characteristics of the final production chips, which have not been publicly benchmarked against existing alternatives.
