Ukrainian and US delegations concluded two days of talks in Florida on Sunday, with Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy saying afterwards that there were signs a new prisoner-of-war exchange with Russia might take place in the coming period. Zelenskyy described the Florida discussions as constructive, noting that specific next steps within the broader peace process had been discussed, though he did not disclose operational details of the potential exchange or its timeline. "There are indications that further prisoner-of-war exchanges may take place, which would indeed be very good news and confirmation that diplomacy is working," he said.

The talks were led on the US side by special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, President Donald Trump's son-in-law, who has been involved in back-channel and formal diplomacy on the Ukraine conflict since the early weeks of Trump's second term. The Ukrainian delegation was headed by Rustem Umierov, Secretary of the National Security and Defence Council, who said in a statement that the two sides had continued their discussions on the next steps within the negotiations and reaffirmed the importance of maintaining communication channels even as battlefield conditions remained volatile. Russian representatives were not present at the Florida talks, which had originally been expected to take place in Abu Dhabi before the venue was changed.

The Florida meetings represent the latest in a series of contacts between the Trump administration and the Ukrainian government as Washington attempts to advance a diplomatic framework capable of ending the four-year war. Trump has repeatedly expressed a desire to broker a swift ceasefire, though the practical parameters of any sustainable agreement — including territorial questions, security guarantees, and the status of occupied regions — have proved highly resistant to resolution. Previous meeting formats have included discussions in Saudi Arabia and bilateral sessions in Washington, with each encounter producing cautious optimism but no breakthrough agreement.

Zelenskyy has maintained throughout the diplomatic process that Ukraine will not accept any settlement that formalises Russian control over Ukrainian territory, a position that has created persistent tension with Washington's stated preference for a pragmatic compromise that ends active hostilities quickly. In recent weeks, Zelenskyy restated Ukraine's position on elections, saying Kyiv would be ready to hold democratic elections if the United States could secure a two-month ceasefire to allow the necessary preparation of infrastructure and international security guarantees. This was not a new concession but a reiteration of a standing offer that has not yet been taken up by the relevant parties.

The possibility of a new POW exchange, if confirmed, would represent a tangible humanitarian deliverable from the Florida talks and would provide both sides with a political gesture of good faith that falls short of the larger political questions. Previous exchanges, conducted with third-party facilitation, have returned hundreds of prisoners on each side, and the families of those in captivity have maintained consistent pressure on their governments to prioritise this dimension of the diplomacy regardless of the pace of broader peace negotiations. International observers cautiously welcomed Zelenskyy's statement while noting that the distance between the parties on core political questions remains substantial.
