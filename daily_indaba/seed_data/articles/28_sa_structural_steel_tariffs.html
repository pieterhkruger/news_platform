South Africa's Department of Trade, Industry and Competition published an official notice on Friday implementing steep anti-dumping duties on structural steel imports from China and Thailand, following a recommendation by the International Trade Administration Commission of South Africa. The move marks one of the most significant trade remedy actions taken by South Africa in years and has been welcomed by the domestic steel industry as a necessary step to protect local manufacturers and jobs.

ITAC set the anti-dumping duty rate on Chinese structural steel at 74.98 percent, significantly higher than the 52.81 percent duty imposed after dumping was found in a previous investigation in 2024. Thailand was also found to have continued dumping steel into the South African market and faces duties of 20.32 percent, up from 9.12 percent imposed earlier. Trade, Industry and Competition Minister Parks Tau approved the new tariffs for a period of up to five years. The measures take immediate effect upon publication in the Government Gazette.

An ITAC investigation found that approximately 28,800 tonnes of structural steel imports from China and Thailand flooded the domestic market during the 2023 and 2024 financial year, with China accounting for about 65 percent of the total. The commission concluded that the cumulative market share of imports increased sixfold in the 2022/23 financial year and ninefold in 2023/24, representing a sustained and deliberate strategy to undercut local pricing and erode the market position of South African producers. ITAC determined that the cheap imported steel amounted to dumping designed to weaken or destroy domestic competitors.

The South African steel sector has long been under pressure from Chinese overcapacity, with Chinese state-owned enterprises producing vast quantities of steel at prices that local mills, facing high electricity costs and constrained capital, cannot match. The Steel and Engineering Industries Federation of Southern Africa welcomed the decision as a lifeline for producers that have seen output and employment decline sharply over the past decade. Domestic producers ArcelorMittal South Africa and others had lodged formal complaints with ITAC arguing that the dumped imports were causing material injury to their operations.

The tariff action comes amid broader global tensions around China's industrial overcapacity, with the United States and European Union also imposing steep duties on Chinese steel and aluminium products. China's Ministry of Commerce has previously described such measures as protectionist and inconsistent with World Trade Organisation rules, though South Africa's action is grounded in anti-dumping provisions that are permitted under the WTO framework. Industry observers noted that the tariffs would provide temporary relief but that structural competitiveness challenges in the South African steel sector, particularly around electricity costs, would need to be addressed for long-term industry sustainability.
