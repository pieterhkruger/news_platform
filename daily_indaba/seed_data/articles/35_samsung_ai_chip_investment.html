Samsung Electronics announced on Wednesday that it plans to spend more than 110 trillion won, equivalent to approximately $73.3 billion, on chip capacity expansion and research in 2026, committing a record amount of capital to an effort to seize the lead in artificial intelligence semiconductors and close the gap with rivals that have outmanoeuvred it in the high-bandwidth memory market critical to AI workloads. The figure represents a 22 percent increase in capital expenditure compared with 2025 and surpasses the roughly $50 billion earmarked by Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company for its own capital programmes this year.

Samsung Electronics Vice Chairman and CEO Jun Young-hyun identified the rise of agentic AI systems as the primary driver of the spending acceleration. Speaking at the company's annual general meeting in Seoul, he explained that as AI applications evolve from basic language models to more autonomous multi-step reasoning systems, the demand for high-bandwidth memory and advanced logic semiconductors is growing at a pace that requires significant advance investment in manufacturing capacity. The company said it would direct the funds toward chip capacity expansion, cutting-edge research, and improvements to its advanced packaging and fabrication capabilities.

A central focus of the investment is Samsung's high-bandwidth memory programme, which has been outpaced in recent years by SK Hynix's dominant position as the preferred supplier of HBM chips to Nvidia. SK Hynix secured early and exclusive supply agreements with Nvidia for the HBM3E generation of chips that power the latest AI accelerators, leaving Samsung in a trailing position at a time when HBM has become one of the most valuable products in the entire semiconductor industry. Samsung has acknowledged that catching up requires not only manufacturing investment but also improvements in yield and performance qualification that take time to achieve at scale.

Beyond memory chips, Samsung's announcement also touched on its ambitions in logic semiconductors, where its foundry business competes with TSMC for orders from major chip designers including Nvidia, AMD and Qualcomm. Samsung's foundry division has struggled to close the technology and yield gap with TSMC at the leading-edge process nodes, and the company has previously signalled its intention to invest heavily in 2-nanometre and below fabrication technologies. Analysts noted that the $73 billion commitment would need to be deployed efficiently to achieve meaningful results, given that increased spending alone has not historically been sufficient to overcome Samsung's foundry yield challenges.

The company also indicated that it was actively exploring mergers and acquisitions in adjacent technology fields, including robotics, medical technology, automotive electronics, and air-conditioning systems, reflecting a broader strategic intent to diversify beyond semiconductors as the core growth driver. Investors responded cautiously to the announcement, recognising the scale of the commitment while noting that execution risk remains significant. Samsung shares were broadly flat on the day, reflecting a market that has been pricing in heavy capex expectations for some time. Analysts at Bloomberg Intelligence estimated that the investment, if on track, positions Samsung as the single largest capital spender in the global semiconductor industry for 2026.
