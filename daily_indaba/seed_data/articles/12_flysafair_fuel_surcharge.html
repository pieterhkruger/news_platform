FlySafair has announced the introduction of a temporary fuel surcharge on all domestic routes, effective from next week, in response to a sharp rise in jet fuel prices driven by escalating tensions in the Middle East and disruptions to global oil supply chains. The airline confirmed that the surcharge, which will range from R80 to R240 per ticket depending on route length, will be reviewed on a monthly basis and removed once fuel prices stabilise. The move follows similar steps by several other regional carriers in recent weeks.

Jet fuel prices in South Africa have risen by approximately 22 percent over the past six weeks, mirroring the surge in global crude benchmarks. The Brent crude oil price has climbed steadily since reports emerged of increased military activity near the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint through which a significant portion of the world's seaborne oil supply passes. South Africa's fuel costs are further complicated by a weakening rand, which amplifies the local impact of dollar-denominated commodity prices.

FlySafair chief commercial officer Kirby Gordon said the decision to introduce the surcharge was not taken lightly, noting that the airline had absorbed a significant portion of the cost increase before concluding that full absorption was no longer sustainable. Gordon said the airline remained committed to offering competitive fares and that the surcharge was structured as a transparent, separately disclosed cost rather than being embedded in base ticket prices. Passengers who had already booked would not be retroactively charged, he confirmed.

Consumer advocacy groups acknowledged the transparency of the airline's approach but called on the government to accelerate the process of reducing aviation fuel taxes, which add significantly to South Africa's baseline fuel costs relative to many comparable markets. Industry bodies have long argued that high local taxes on aviation fuel put South African carriers at a competitive disadvantage and inflate the cost of air travel for consumers. The Department of Transport confirmed it was monitoring the fuel cost situation but did not indicate any immediate policy response.
