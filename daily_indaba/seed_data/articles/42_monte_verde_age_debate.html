A new study published in the journal Science has proposed that the Monte Verde archaeological site in southern Chile, long celebrated as one of the oldest known human occupation sites in the Americas and a cornerstone of debates about when and how the first people arrived in the New World, may be significantly younger than previously established. The research, led by Professor Todd Surovell of the University of Wyoming in collaboration with colleagues from Chile, Austria, and the US Geological Survey, suggests the site is between 4,200 and 8,200 years old — far more recent than the approximately 14,500 years posited by the researchers who excavated and studied Monte Verde from the 1970s through the 1990s.

The central argument of the new study is that the radiocarbon dates that gave Monte Verde its famous antiquity were likely derived from redeposited Ice Age wood rather than from materials directly associated with human occupation. The site lies along a creek, and the researchers found that the surrounding deposits contain abundant Ice Age organic material — wood and other plant remains from the last glacial period — that was eroded and transported into the same sedimentary layers that also contain artefacts attributable to human activity. The mixing of these materials, Surovell and colleagues argue, produced anomalously old radiocarbon dates that do not reflect the actual age of human presence at the site.

The implications of this reinterpretation are profound. Monte Verde's claimed age of 14,500 years made it one of the primary pieces of evidence for a pre-Clovis human presence in the Americas — that is, the idea that people inhabited South America before the Clovis culture, which appears in the North American archaeological record around 13,000 years ago. The pre-Clovis hypothesis, which challenges the earlier dominant view that Clovis people were the first Americans, has been supported by Monte Verde and a handful of other contested sites. If Monte Verde's occupation was actually more recent, it would remove one of the anchor points for this model.

The study has already drawn sharp criticism from Tom Dillehay, the archaeologist who led the original Monte Verde excavations and whose decades of meticulous fieldwork established the site's international reputation. Dillehay strongly rejected the study's conclusions, arguing that the new researchers had misinterpreted the site's stratigraphy and methodology, and that the redeposition hypothesis failed to account for the spatial associations between the artefacts and the dated materials in the original excavation data. His objections were echoed by other specialists in the pre-Clovis field, who raised methodological concerns about the sampling approach used in the new study.

The Monte Verde debate reflects broader unresolved questions about the peopling of the Americas that have persisted in the archaeological literature for decades. New genetic and isotopic studies have complicated the picture further, suggesting that the first Americans may have arrived by multiple routes at different times, including both overland and coastal pathways. The University of Wyoming-led study will not settle the controversy — Surovell and Dillehay are both respected authorities whose disagreement signals a genuine empirical dispute — but it has reopened a discussion that some had considered effectively closed, and it is likely to prompt new fieldwork designed to resolve the dating questions at Monte Verde with higher-resolution methods.
