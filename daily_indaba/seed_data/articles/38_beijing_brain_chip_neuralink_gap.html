China's drug regulator said on March 13 that it had approved the sale of a brain-computer interface system designed to help restore hand movement, in what Reuters described as the world's first approval of a BCI device for commercial use. The product was developed by Borui Kang Medical Technology in Shanghai and was cleared by the National Medical Products Administration.

The regulator said the system is intended for patients with quadriplegia caused by cervical spinal cord injuries. It is designed to help eligible users regain hand-grasping ability through a glove linked to the implanted system. Reuters reported that the product is classified as an invasive BCI, while the company and regulator described the implantation approach as minimally invasive and wireless.

Eligibility is tightly defined. Patients must be between 18 and 60 years old, have a qualifying spinal-cord injury diagnosis that is at least a year old, remain clinically stable for six months after standard treatment, and be unable to grasp with their hands while still retaining some upper-arm function. According to the regulator, clinical-trial data showed significant gains in hand-grasping ability and an associated improvement in quality of life for participants.

The approval adds concrete weight to Beijing's push to turn brain-computer interfaces into a strategic growth industry. The NMPA said BCI products had been prioritised, and Reuters noted that the sector was named a "future industry" in China's latest five-year plan released earlier in March. In separate Reuters reporting that week, brain-science researcher Yao Dezhong said China could see practical public use of BCI products within three to five years as the technology matures.

The commercial clearance also highlights the speed of China's broader BCI push. While US groups such as Neuralink remain the best-known players globally, Chinese researchers and manufacturers are advancing across invasive, semi-invasive and non-invasive systems aimed primarily at rehabilitation and clinical care. For now, the approved Shanghai device is narrowly targeted, but it marks a real shift from laboratory and trial work into regulated medical use.
