Bryson DeChambeau claimed the LIV Golf South Africa title at the Club at Steyn City on Sunday after defeating Jon Rahm on the first hole of a sudden-death playoff, completing a comeback from a tense final round in which his Crushers GC team surrendered a seemingly commanding lead to the South Africa-backed Southern Guards before ultimately holding on. The victory was DeChambeau's second consecutive LIV Golf win and the fifth individual title of his LIV career, and it drew tears from the American as the significance of winning in front of the passionate South African crowd became clear.

DeChambeau and Rahm were level after 54 holes and returned to the 18th, a par-5, for the playoff. DeChambeau made a decisive statement on the first extra hole, smashing a 3-wood from a difficult wet lie in the rough to set up a birdie, while Rahm could only match par. The winning birdie prompted an eruption from the large crowd that had stayed beyond the final round's conclusion to witness the decider. "Thank you, thank you, thank you," DeChambeau said to the crowd in three words that captured the emotional pitch of the moment.

The team competition provided the day's dramatic narrative arc. DeChambeau's Crushers GC were trailing the Southern Guards, the team composed largely of South African players and fan favourites, by as many as nine shots at one point during Sunday's final round, with the home crowd roaring on every Southern Guards birdie. But the Crushers mounted a remarkable back-nine recovery, eventually winning the team prize by a single stroke and claiming the ninth regular season team title in LIV Golf history, the most by any team in the league. The Southern Guards, who had appeared on the verge of a breakthrough victory on home soil, were gracious in defeat to a chorus of supportive applause from the gallery.

DeChambeau's individual prize of $4 million, combined with the Crushers' $3 million team payout split among four players, made this one of the most lucrative weeks of competitive golf seen in the southern hemisphere. The total purse for the event was $30 million, the standard LIV Golf prize fund. Golf Digest reported the full prize money breakdown across all 48 individual competitors and all 12 team formations, with significant earnings spread through the field. South Africa's own Charl Schwartzel and Branden Grace banked individual placing prize money from a combined performance that also generated considerable domestic pride.

The victory further consolidated DeChambeau's status as one of LIV Golf's signature figures. The win came against a world-class field and under intense pressure, validating the belief within the LIV ecosystem that the circuit is capable of producing elite competitive golf. For South African fans, the tournament served as a powerful reminder of the country's deep love for the game and its ability to host major events on the global circuit. LIV Golf announced after the event that South Africa would return to the 2027 schedule, confirming that the inaugural visit had more than justified the organisers' investment in the continent.
