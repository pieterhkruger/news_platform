NASA's Perseverance rover has gathered radar data confirming that ancient lake sediments were deposited by water inside Mars' Jezero Crater, according to a study published in Science Advances in January 2024. Reuters reported that the work was led by teams from UCLA and the University of Oslo and strengthens the case that Jezero once held a substantial body of water fed by a river delta.

The rover made the measurements with its RIMFAX ground-penetrating radar instrument as it crossed the western edge of the crater and the ancient fan-shaped delta. The radar readings showed layered material and erosion surfaces beneath the ground that match the kinds of sedimentary deposits formed when a river empties into a lake. The data reached roughly 20 metres below the surface.

The finding helped resolve a question that had lingered since Perseverance landed in 2021. Scientists had expected to find sedimentary rock on the crater floor because Jezero had long been identified from orbit as an ancient lake basin, but the rover initially encountered volcanic rock instead. The subsurface radar results showed that the expected lake-and-delta history was still preserved in the area the rover later explored.

That matters because lake sediments are among the best places to look for signs that a world may once have supported life. Fine-grained deposits can preserve chemical traces and textures far better than many other rock types. The Jezero results therefore give added scientific weight to the samples Perseverance has collected and to the long-running goal of one day returning them to Earth for laboratory study.

The January 2024 study did not claim to have found life. What it did provide was a clearer reconstruction of Jezero's past: a crater that once contained standing water, received sediment from flowing rivers, and preserved that history beneath the Martian surface for billions of years. For planetary scientists, that is exactly the kind of environment worth interrogating in detail.
