Pop Mart, the Chinese designer toy company behind the globally viral Labubu figure, and Sony Pictures Entertainment have officially announced a partnership to develop a Labubu feature film, with Wonka director Paul King attached to direct, produce, and co-write the script. The announcement was made at an event tied to the Monsters' 10th-anniversary global exhibition tour in Paris, providing a suitably theatrical backdrop for a project that seeks to translate one of consumer culture's most recognisable characters into a major cinematic property.

Paul King, whose direction of Wonka generated more than $635 million at the global box office in 2023, will collaborate on the screenplay with screenwriter Steven Levenson. Labubu creator Kasing Lung, the Hong Kong-based artist and illustrator who originally conceived the elf-like character with its distinctive serrated teeth and pointed ears as part of his Forest Tales series, will serve as executive producer on the project, ensuring creative continuity between the toy's original artistic vision and its cinematic adaptation. The film is being developed as a live-action and CGI hybrid, a format well-suited to translating the toy's three-dimensional, tactile appeal into a photoreal but fantastical screen environment.

Pop Mart is investing in the film at a moment when the Labubu craze, which ignited globally after being popularised on social media by celebrities including K-pop star Lisa of BLACKPINK, has shown signs of cooling from its peak frenzy. The Bloomberg report that surfaced the news noted that the company is betting a cinematic narrative can revive and sustain interest in the character as the blind-box collectible market becomes more competitive and consumer novelty begins to fade. A major film backed by Sony would provide narrative depth that toys alone cannot offer and would introduce the character to audiences in markets where the physical product has not yet penetrated.

Sony Pictures' involvement signals significant confidence in Labubu's mainstream crossover potential. The studio has demonstrated a consistent willingness to back franchise projects rooted in non-Western intellectual property, and its distribution network would position the Labubu film for broad global release rather than the limited art house or animation festival circuits. The project is currently in the early stages of development, with no release date announced, but the attachment of Paul King — who has built a reputation for bringing beloved characters to screen with warmth, inventiveness, and commercial appeal — suggests a serious commitment to quality.

The broader cultural moment for the film is notable. Labubu has become one of the defining objects of a particular strain of contemporary consumer culture — one that blends collectibility, artistic credibility, celebrity endorsement, and social media virality into something that transcends the category of a simple toy. Whether that cultural resonance can be translated into a durable cinematic franchise will depend on the story the filmmakers choose to tell and on whether the character's appeal extends beyond its physical and aesthetic charm to support a narrative world. The Pop Mart-Sony partnership gives the project the resources to find out.
