President Cyril Ramaphosa has formally authorised the deployment of 2,200 members of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) to assist police in a nationwide crackdown on illegal mining and organised gang violence. The deployment, which falls under the banner of Operation Prosper, will see military personnel stationed across five provinces until the end of March 2027. The intervention arrives amid mounting public frustration over violent crime and the sustained inability of civilian law enforcement to independently stabilise high-risk areas.

In his constitutional notice to Parliament, the President confirmed that the prolonged military operation carries an estimated cost of R823 million. Troops will operate as a force multiplier for the South African Police Service (SAPS) in Gauteng, the Western Cape, the Eastern Cape, the North West, and the Free State. Rather than taking the lead on investigations, soldiers will provide tactical security during high-risk police raids, secure critical public infrastructure, and conduct cordon-and-search operations to dismantle entrenched criminal syndicates.

The operational rollout executes a core security pledge made during Ramaphosa's recent State of the Nation Address. The realities of this directive became visible earlier this week when armoured military convoys entered Johannesburg suburbs, including Riverlea and Westbury, to dismantle illegal mining sites and suppress gang hostilities. While the aggressive show of state authority has drawn immediate relief from crime-weary residents, the militarisation of civilian spaces remains a heavily scrutinised practice given South Africa's historical context.

Anticipating constitutional and historical anxieties, the Presidency has stressed that the armed forces will operate strictly under civilian police command with explicitly defined rules of engagement. Despite these assurances, domestic security analysts caution against viewing the deployment as a permanent solution. Experts warn that unless the temporary suppression of street-level violence is paired with rigorous intelligence gathering, anti-corruption efforts within the police force, and successful prosecutions, the targeted criminal networks will simply adapt and outlast the military presence.
