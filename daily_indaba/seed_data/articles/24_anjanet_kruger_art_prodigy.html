At just sixteen years old, Anjanet Krüger is producing work that experienced South African artists describe as extraordinary — a loose, impressionist style marked by a confidence and emotional depth that most painters take decades to develop. Working from her home studio, she has quietly assembled a body of work that is drawing serious attention from galleries, art educators and fellow artists who recognise in her something genuinely rare: instinctive talent that has arrived, as far as anyone can tell, almost fully formed.

Anjanet's primary medium is traditional painting, where her signature approach places her in the tradition of painters like Sorolla and early Monet. The impressionist impulse is unmistakably her own, however. She paints with a loose, gestural application that captures mood and movement rather than strict realism — and she paints animals with particular emotional intelligence. Portraits of her family dog have earned admiring commentary from professional artists who encountered them online, struck by the way each stroke carries weight without belaboring the subject. The looseness never tips into carelessness. There is a quiet authority in the mark-making that suggests deep observational skill and a painter who has spent far more time looking than most teenagers her age.

What sets Anjanet apart from many talented young traditional painters is her parallel mastery of the digital medium — and the fact that she arrived there entirely on her own. Self-taught on Krita, the professional open-source illustration and animation platform, she has developed a digital painting vocabulary that mirrors and extends her traditional practice. She also uses Krita to create character animations in a style reminiscent of classic Disney hand-drawn production: fluid, expressive, and technically ambitious in ways that stop experienced animators when they encounter them. The animations have attracted a following in online creative communities, where artists and animators familiar with the labour involved find the quality striking regardless of the creator's age.

Art educators who have reviewed Anjanet's portfolio emphasise the significance of her self-directed development. In an era where digital tools are increasingly accessible, many young people engage with creative software at a surface level. Anjanet has approached it with the same seriousness she brings to painting — studying the tools methodically, developing consistent technique, and building an aesthetic sensibility that runs coherently across both mediums. The result is a body of work that reads not as the output of a student experimenting but of an artist who already knows what she wants to say and how she wants to say it. That kind of clarity is unusual at any age.

Gallery owners and art commentators in South Africa who have seen her work note that loose, impressionist-influenced painting is a style that rewards technical proficiency but demands emotional honesty — and that Anjanet appears to have both in unusual measure. Several describe her as one of the most compelling young talents they have encountered in recent years. Whether she pursues formal art education, a career in animation, or an independent creative path remains to be seen. What is already clear is that South Africa has produced a talent that deserves far wider recognition than it has yet received, and that the art world would do well to pay attention before the rest of the country catches up.
