South Africa's National Treasury said on Tuesday that it expected government debt to stabilise as planned despite surging oil prices and the market volatility triggered by the conflict in the Middle East, pointing to the potential offset from stronger export commodity prices as a key stabilising factor. The statement sought to reassure financial markets that the country's debt trajectory remained intact amid global turbulence that has rattled currencies, tightened credit conditions and raised the spectre of renewed inflationary pressure.

Treasury Director-General Duncan Pieterse acknowledged that the oil price spike, with Brent crude trading above $100 per barrel for the third week in a row, was a significant headwind for South Africa as a net energy importer. However, he emphasised that the broader impact of shifting commodity prices on the country's terms of trade was more relevant to the fiscal outlook than the crude oil price in isolation. South Africa's major export commodities — gold, platinum group metals, coal and iron ore — have all strengthened significantly in recent weeks, with gold in particular surging above $3,200 per ounce on safe-haven demand linked to the conflict.

The February 2026 budget forecast that gross debt would stabilise at around 76.2 percent of GDP in the current fiscal year, before embarking on a gradual downward path. Pieterse noted that the strong performance of commodity revenues was supporting tax receipts beyond initial projections, providing a partial natural hedge against the fiscal pressure of higher oil import costs. Plans to introduce a fiscal anchor, which would give legal backing to the debt stabilisation target, are expected to be tabled later in 2026.

Not all analysts are convinced by the reassuring tone. Several economists pointed out that at 78.9 percent debt-to-GDP by some broader measures, and with a deficit still running at around 4.5 percent of GDP, South Africa's fiscal buffers are limited. Every sustained $10 increase in the oil price is estimated to reduce South African GDP growth by approximately 20 basis points, compounding the challenge of reviving an economy that grew by only 1.1 percent in 2025. Business Day reported that a prolonged period of elevated oil prices would place significant strain on South Africa's still fragile recovery.

The volatility in oil markets and its implications for inflation have clouded the prospects for interest rate cuts, which had been widely anticipated earlier in the year when the SARB achieved its new 3 percent inflation target. Some economists now see a meaningful probability that rates will be held for longer than previously expected, and a minority view points to a possible increase at the tail end of 2026 if inflation re-accelerates. Treasury officials stressed that the government remained committed to fiscal consolidation and that contingency measures would be reviewed if oil prices remained elevated beyond the current quarter.
