African airlines are facing the steepest operating cost pressures in years as global jet fuel prices surge in the wake of the Middle East conflict, with the partial closure of the Strait of Hormuz disrupting the supply chains on which much of Africa's aviation fuel ultimately depends. Industry bodies warn that the cost shock could force fare increases, route suspensions and a wave of financial distress among carriers that were only recently recovering from pandemic-related losses.

Jet fuel prices in north-west Europe have surged to near $239 per barrel since the conflict began, more than double their level at the start of January, and the impact has been felt disproportionately across the African continent. The African Airlines Association has pointed out that jet fuel typically accounts for between 30 and 40 percent of operating costs for African carriers, compared with a global average of 20 to 25 percent, owing to the region's lower aircraft efficiency, older fleets, and thinner route networks. For low-cost carriers, the burden is even heavier: South Africa's FlySafair stated that jet fuel makes up between 50 and 55 percent of its direct operating costs.

Nearly 70 percent of Africa's aviation fuel imports are estimated to pass through or be priced against supply chains that pass through the Strait of Hormuz or the broader Gulf region, making the continent particularly exposed to the current disruption. FlySafair announced in early March the introduction of a temporary fuel surcharge on certain routes, the first such measure by a South African low-cost carrier in several years, citing the extraordinary pressure from oil markets. Kenya Airways has likewise announced fare increases to offset rising costs, while Ethiopian Airlines, one of the continent's largest carriers, has warned of capacity reductions on its Middle East routes following the suspension of flights to several regional destinations.

The International Air Transport Association has noted that African airlines entered the current crisis with relatively thin cash reserves, having only recently returned to profitability after the combined shocks of the COVID-19 pandemic and the post-pandemic recovery challenges. Several smaller national carriers on the continent were already in financial difficulty before the fuel spike, raising concerns that some may not survive an extended period of elevated costs. Analysts at aviation consultancies have called for African governments to consider temporary fuel levy relief or credit guarantee schemes to help carriers bridge the period of acute price pressure.

The broader knock-on effects extend to tourism, trade and connectivity across the continent. Higher airfares reduce passenger volumes on leisure routes, while cargo rate increases push up the cost of importing time-sensitive goods such as pharmaceuticals, fresh produce, and manufactured parts. Some regional routes that were only marginally profitable at lower fuel prices are at risk of being suspended, which would reduce connectivity in markets where air travel is often the only practical option for reaching remote communities. The aviation industry has urged the international community to prioritise restoring stability to Middle East shipping lanes, not only for energy markets but for the wider global logistics system.
