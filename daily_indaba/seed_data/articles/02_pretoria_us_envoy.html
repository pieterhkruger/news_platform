The Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) has formally summoned the United States Ambassador to South Africa, Leo Brent Bozell III, to seek clarification following remarks the government deemed “undiplomatic” and an infringement on national sovereignty. The demarche, issued this week, follows a public address by Bozell at a business conference where he sharply criticized a range of South African domestic policies and its independent foreign policy positions. The remarks have plunged bilateral relations to a new low, reflecting the heightened tension since the political shift in Washington.

A primary point of contention was Bozell's commentary on South Africa's judiciary and a sensitive historical chant. He reportedly dismissed a court ruling that found an apartheid-era liberation struggle chant did not constitute hate speech in its political context, stating that he did not care what the courts said and considered it hate speech. While the US embassy later issued a statement clarifying that the US government respects the independence and findings of the South African judiciary, the initial public dismissal caused deep offence, with DIRCO officials stating that it “undermined the integrity” of the country's judicial institutions.

The ambassador also used the platform to attack core domestic transformation policies, describing Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment and land expropriation legislation as “anti-white.” He compared the former to apartheid-era racial laws, a comparison the South African government vehemently rejected as a “fundamental misrepresentation.” Minister of International Relations and Cooperation, Ronald Lamola, reasserted that these policies are essential, constitutionally-mandated instruments to address the “structural imbalances” and historical injustices inherited from colonialism and apartheid.

Beyond domestic affairs, Bozell issued a public warning that Washington's patience was “running out” over Pretoria's foreign policy alignments, specifically its diplomatic ties with Iran. He mentioned a list of “five asks” the US administration had presented a year ago to rebuild relations, noting that a response was still outstanding. This public ultimatum, combined with existing US actions such as the expulsion of the South African ambassador to Washington last year and a new, broad-based trade investigation, is seen in Pretoria as part of a campaign of coordinated pressure aimed at coercing a sovereign state into amending its global partnerships.

Not all observers have accepted this framing, however. A number of commentators — particularly those sympathetic to the current administration in Washington or sceptical of the South African media’s ideological disposition — have characterised the coverage as an exercise in leftist politics, aimed at portraying the United States in the most damaging light possible. Their argument is that outlets broadly hostile to the Trump administration’s foreign policy agenda applied selective editorial judgement: choosing to foreground the most incendiary moments of a far longer engagement while treating its substantive content as unreportable. In their reading, the framing of the visit as a diplomatic incident is itself a political act — one shaped less by what Bozell actually said than by what certain editors wanted him to have said.

A review of the full address lends some support to that argument. By most accounts, the prepared remarks were broadly constructive. Bozell spoke at length about the depth of economic partnership between the two countries, the importance of American private-sector investment in South Africa’s infrastructure and energy transition, and the shared commitment to democratic norms and rule of law. He praised South African entrepreneurs and civil society organisations and described the bilateral relationship as one of enduring strategic value. His supporters contend that this substantive content — which represented the bulk of his address — was almost entirely absent from the subsequent reporting, which compressed a complex hour-long engagement into a handful of contested Q&A exchanges. Whether or not that editorial prioritisation was deliberate, its effect was to define the visit entirely by its flashpoints.

Perhaps the most philosophically layered element of the controversy surrounds the “Kill the Boer” comment. South African courts have adjudicated the matter in multiple proceedings, finding that in its historical political context the chant does not constitute hate speech — a finding the government is both entitled and obligated to defend. What Bozell expressed during questioning, however, was a personal moral view: that regardless of any court’s conclusion, he personally considered the chant hate speech. This is a position he is entitled to hold. Free expression — the foundational value invoked most insistently by those who share his broader politics — must by its own logic extend to the right to disagree with judicial interpretations of contested speech. The distinction that matters diplomatically is not whether he may hold that view, but whether volunteering it unprompted at a public business forum, as a serving ambassador, reflected sound professional judgement. On that narrower question, even some of those sympathetic to his broader critique of South African policy have conceded the answer was no.
