South Africa's economy grew by 1.1 percent in 2025, falling short of the National Treasury's forecast of 1.7 percent and the International Monetary Fund's projection of 1.5 percent. The latest figures, released by Statistics South Africa, confirm that the country continues to face structural headwinds that are suppressing growth well below the levels needed to meaningfully reduce unemployment. The underperformance comes despite a marked improvement in electricity supply following years of severe load shedding, suggesting that power alone is not sufficient to unlock faster economic expansion.

The agriculture sector provided one of the brighter points in the data, growing strongly off the back of improved rainfall and higher commodity prices. Mining output also recovered modestly as Transnet rail and port performance stabilised after years of logistical dysfunction. However, the manufacturing, construction and retail sectors remained subdued, with consumers under pressure from elevated interest rates and persistently high food and fuel costs throughout much of the year.

Treasury officials acknowledged the miss but argued that the downward revision reflected global conditions as much as domestic policy failures. Weaker demand from key trading partners, including China and Germany, weighed on export volumes, while rising input costs squeezed margins across several industries. The rand's volatility during periods of international uncertainty also complicated business planning and investment decisions.

Economists say the 2025 figures reinforce the urgency of structural reforms, particularly in network industries such as energy, rail and water. Business groups have long argued that reliable infrastructure is the single biggest constraint on private investment. Without measurable progress on logistics and regulatory efficiency, analysts warn that South Africa risks another year of sub-potential growth in 2026, keeping unemployment near its record highs.
