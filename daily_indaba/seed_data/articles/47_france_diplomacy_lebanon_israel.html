French Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot travelled to Israel on Friday in an unscheduled visit following a day of talks in Beirut on Thursday, in a stepped-up French diplomatic effort to reduce regional tensions and advance prospects for a ceasefire in the Lebanon conflict. The back-to-back visits to Beirut and Jerusalem underlined France's increasingly assertive role as a mediator between Israel and Lebanon at a moment when wider Middle East hostilities, including the ongoing war involving Iran, have heightened the risk of further regional escalation.

In Beirut, Barrot met Lebanese President Joseph Aoun, Prime Minister Nawaf Salam, and Parliament Speaker Nabih Berri. The French foreign ministry said Barrot conveyed France's solidarity with Lebanon and discussed both the security situation along the Lebanese-Israeli border and the significant humanitarian challenges facing the country. Lebanese officials said that Lebanon was prepared to form a negotiating delegation to engage in potential talks with Israel on the basis of a framework that respected Lebanese sovereignty and complied with international law, a formulation that left the terms of engagement deliberately broad.

The Paris-based diplomatic effort has included a detailed French peace proposal that has circulated among the parties and their international sponsors in recent weeks. According to reports confirmed by French diplomatic sources, the proposal envisages Lebanon taking the historically unprecedented step of recognising Israel in exchange for a negotiated political declaration, with the United States and France co-sponsoring the process. A final phase of the French plan would see the demarcation of borders between Israel and Lebanon, and between Lebanon and Syria, by the end of 2026. Israel has so far rebuffed the outline, describing Beirut's offered terms as insufficient, while Lebanon has indicated conditional openness to discussions.

The United States, which has been pursuing its own parallel diplomatic track with both parties, has been lukewarm on aspects of the French proposals, though discussions between Washington and Paris have continued. The Biden-era framework for Lebanon-Israel stabilisation was carried forward and modified under the Trump administration's special envoy team, and there are reported tensions between Washington and Paris about the respective pace and sequencing of diplomatic efforts. France has historically maintained close ties with Lebanon and sees itself as a special guarantor of the country's sovereignty and the wellbeing of its Christian communities, a self-assigned role that gives Paris particular standing in Lebanese affairs but also complicates coordination with other Western partners.

Analysts noted that the timing of Barrot's dual visit — to Beirut and then Jerusalem within 24 hours — sent a deliberate signal of balanced engagement, showing that France was not acting as a partisan advocate for either side but as a genuine intermediary. The French approach has emphasised humanitarian openings as a pathway to building confidence for deeper political discussions, and Barrot raised both the question of Israeli military positions in Lebanese territory and the need for expanded humanitarian access to civilian populations in southern Lebanon during his meetings in both capitals. Whether the shuttle diplomacy produces any tangible progress toward a negotiated framework remains to be seen, with both sides far apart on core questions.
