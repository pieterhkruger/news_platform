South Africa has no reason to cut ties with Iran, the country's director-general of international relations and cooperation said on Monday, after the new US ambassador was quoted describing the country's association with the Islamic Republic as an impediment to good relations with Washington. The statement by Zane Dangor marks the most direct public rejection yet by Pretoria of Trump administration demands related to South Africa's foreign policy alignments.

In an interview with Reuters, Dangor also rejected several other positions attributed to the Trump administration, including demands that South Africa drop its genocide case against Israel at the International Court of Justice, scrap black economic empowerment legislation, or participate in a refugee programme for white South Africans. He emphasised that Pretoria remained willing to improve relations with Washington, but only on the basis of areas of genuine agreement. "You have a particular relationship with Iran that many in the developing world do not have," Dangor said, referring to the US.

The remarks came against the backdrop of the US-led military campaign against Iran, now entering its fourth week, which has placed acute pressure on governments across the developing world to publicly distance themselves from Tehran. South Africa, a member of BRICS and a historically non-aligned state, has maintained diplomatic ties with Iran for decades and has consistently opposed what it characterises as coercive unilateralism in international relations. New US Ambassador Leo Bozell had told News24 in his first media interview that South Africa's ties to Iran posed a problem for bilateral relations.

The South African government has been navigating a difficult international environment since President Donald Trump's return to office in January 2025, with relations between Pretoria and Washington deteriorating sharply. US officials have raised objections to South Africa's land reform programme, its legal action against Israel, and its BRICS membership. Dangor signalled on Monday that while South Africa values its economic relationship with the United States, it would not compromise its independent foreign policy stance under pressure.

Analysts note that South Africa's position is consistent with its longstanding commitment to multilateralism and its historical ties to liberation movements that received support from a range of global partners during the apartheid era, including Iran. The government of President Cyril Ramaphosa has repeatedly stated that it does not take sides in international conflicts and opposes the application of Western-led sanctions regimes that it was not party to negotiating. The standoff with Washington is expected to cast a shadow over trade discussions, with South Africa's continued access to preferential terms under the African Growth and Opportunity Act among the key economic considerations.
