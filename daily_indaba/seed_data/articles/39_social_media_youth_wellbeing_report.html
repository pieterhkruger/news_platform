Heavy use of social media is contributing to a stark and measurable decline in the wellbeing of young people, particularly teenagers, according to the World Happiness Report 2026, released this week by the Wellbeing Research Centre at Oxford University. The report, which analyses happiness and life satisfaction trends across more than 150 countries, identifies social media as a key driver of the significant gap that has opened between the wellbeing of young people and older generations in many high-income countries over the past decade.

The findings show that life evaluations among people under 25 in the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand have dropped substantially since the mid-2010s, with the report's authors identifying long daily hours of social media use as a significant contributing factor. Girls are particularly affected: 15-year-old girls who use social media platforms for more than five hours a day report markedly lower life satisfaction compared with their peers who use these platforms less intensively. Heavy users, defined in the report as those spending more than seven hours a day on social media, are identified as being at especially high risk of negative wellbeing outcomes including anxiety, depression, and reduced sense of social connection.

The report's researchers found that the most harmful platforms share common characteristics: they are driven by algorithmic recommendation feeds, feature influencer content prominently, and centre on visual material that promotes social comparison. Platforms that primarily facilitate direct communication between people show a weaker negative association with wellbeing. The distinction points to the nature of the interaction — passive consumption of curated content versus active social communication — as an important mediating factor in social media's effects on mental health.

Not all regions show the same pattern. In eight of the ten global regions studied, those in the youngest age group report higher life evaluations today than in the baseline period of 2006 to 2010. The negative trend is concentrated in English-speaking Western countries and Western Europe, where smartphone adoption and social media use are deepest and where the algorithmic content ecosystem is most developed. Researchers caution that correlation does not definitively establish causation, but note that the consistency of the pattern across multiple data sources and time periods strengthens the case for treating social media exposure as a genuine risk factor for youth wellbeing.

The findings have already prompted policy responses in several countries. Australia raised the minimum age for access to many major social media platforms to 16 at the end of 2025, and similar legislation is under active consideration in Germany, Denmark, France, and Spain. The World Happiness Report's authors stopped short of prescribing a single universal policy solution, instead calling for governments to treat social media's effects on young people as a serious public health concern warranting rigorous regulation, transparent algorithmic standards, and sustained investment in alternative activities and social infrastructure for adolescents.
