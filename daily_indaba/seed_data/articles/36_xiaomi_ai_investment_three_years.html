Xiaomi's CEO Lei Jun announced on Wednesday that the Chinese smartphone and electric vehicle maker would invest at least 60 billion yuan, equivalent to approximately $8.7 billion, in artificial intelligence over the next three years, signalling a dramatic expansion of the company's technology ambitions beyond its established consumer hardware business. The announcement, made at a company event in Beijing, drove Xiaomi's Hong Kong-listed shares up more than 5 percent on the day.

The announcement came a day after Xiaomi formally unveiled three new large AI models under the MiMo brand: the flagship base model MiMo-V2-Pro, a comprehensive agent-capable version called MiMo-V2-Omni, and a text-to-audio model designated MiMo-V2-TTS. Xiaomi said the MiMo-V2-Pro has already processed more than 1.5 trillion tokens since its release and has risen to prominent positions on major AI benchmarking leaderboards. The company framed the model launch and the investment commitment as complementary elements of a coherent AI strategy, in which its consumer hardware — smartphones, tablets, electric vehicles and home appliances — serve as a deployment platform for AI-powered features and services.

Xiaomi also revealed that MiClaw, its first smartphone AI agent, has entered closed beta testing. The agent is designed to operate autonomously across the phone's functions, completing multi-step tasks on the user's behalf by drawing on the MiMo large models and integrating deeply with Xiaomi's HyperOS operating system and its broader people-car-home smart ecosystem. Analysts view agents as the next major commercial frontier in consumer AI, with Xiaomi's integrated hardware and software stack positioning it well to compete with domestic rivals including Huawei and Oppo in this emerging category.

The investment announcement positions Xiaomi alongside other major Chinese technology companies that have recently pledged substantial AI capital commitments, including Tencent, Alibaba and Baidu. China's government has provided significant policy support for domestic AI development, partly in response to US export controls that have restricted access to the most advanced Nvidia chips, prompting Chinese companies to invest more heavily in domestic chip alternatives and software-level efficiency gains. Xiaomi has been working with Chinese chip designers to optimise its models for domestically available hardware, reducing dependency on restricted foreign components.

The three-year timeline for the $8.7 billion commitment suggests an average annual AI spending rate of nearly $3 billion, a significant acceleration from the company's previous technology investment profile. Lei Jun has positioned AI as central to Xiaomi's long-term differentiation strategy, arguing that the convergence of AI with electric vehicles, smart home devices and mobile computing will create a new category of deeply integrated user experiences that existing players cannot easily replicate. Investors and analysts have generally responded positively to the clarity of the strategic commitment, though questions remain about the pace at which Xiaomi can translate AI investment into measurable improvements in revenue and margin.
