South Africa's annual consumer price inflation slowed to 3.0 percent in February, matching the South African Reserve Bank's newly adopted target for the first time since the government and central bank lowered the official goal from a 3–6 percent band to a midpoint of 3 percent in the November 2025 Medium Term Budget Policy Statement. Statistics South Africa released the figures on Wednesday, with the data pointing to broad-based disinflation driven largely by declining fuel and transport costs.

The data showed that the continued slowdown was led by a 2.1 percent drop in transportation prices, reflecting a sharper decline in fuels of 10.1 percent year on year, while food price increases also eased. Core inflation, which strips out volatile food and energy components, similarly fell to 3.0 percent. The SARB Governor Lesetja Kganyago had in January said the bank was on course to meet the new 3 percent target in 2026, and February's reading confirms that trajectory, at least for now.

The relief from the inflation data was tempered, however, by the rising risks associated with the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran. The partial blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has pushed Brent crude oil above $100 per barrel for the first time since 2022, creating the conditions for a sharp reversal in South Africa's fuel cost trajectory in the coming months. Economists warn that if elevated oil prices are sustained through the second quarter, transport and energy inflation could surge, potentially pushing headline CPI back above the 3 percent target.

South Africa imports the bulk of its petroleum and remains acutely sensitive to global oil price movements, with petrol price adjustments feeding rapidly into food prices, logistics costs and broader services inflation. Analysts at several local investment banks have revised their full-year inflation forecasts upward to a range of 3.5 to 4.2 percent, contingent on the duration and severity of the Middle East disruptions. The SARB's Monetary Policy Committee is scheduled to meet on 26 March, and the evolving inflation picture has complicated the calculus around the timing and pace of potential interest rate adjustments.

The rand's weakening trend over recent weeks, driven in part by global risk-off sentiment and South Africa-specific political tensions with the United States, adds a further upside inflation risk. A weaker currency raises the cost of all imported goods and could partially offset the domestic disinflationary forces that brought inflation to target in February. Whether the country can hold inflation at 3 percent through the second half of 2026 will depend heavily on how quickly the situation in the Middle East is resolved and whether oil supply disruptions ease.
